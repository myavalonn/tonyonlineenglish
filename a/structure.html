One technology that has come a long way in recent years is eye tracking. Advancements in machine learning and cameras have made tracking someone’s gaze easier than ever before. 21

Eye tracking technology works by first projecting a pattern of light onto a person’s eyes. Cameras then take high-resolution images of the eyes and the pattern. This information is then processed using complex algorithms, allowing the computer to work out the person’s gaze point.

The most obvious use of such technology is by people who are unable to use their hands. 22 The technology could also be used by anyone to control these kinds of devices at a distance, for example, in a smart home where appliances could be controlled from across the room.

( D ) Example: Another industry that can benefit from the technology is advertising.

However, there are also concerns about the potential misuse of this technology. Sophisticated eye tracking can infer private information about people, including their interests and skills, and even their mental health situation. 23 Should such information be gathered and used without a person’s permission, it would constitute a serious breach of privacy.

The development of ever more sophisticated eye-tracking technology is certainly exciting. 24 Like any technology, eye-tracking can be abused. 25

The horseshoe crab may be one of the ocean’s more odder-looking creatures, but few would consider it particularly valuable. 21 If people knew why, they would undoubtedly gain a new appreciation for this ancient species.

Since the 1970s, horseshoe crab blood has been used to detect the presence of potentially deadly bacteria in medical products and drugs. 22 Everything from pacemakers to COVID-19 vaccines is tested for toxins in this way, as are the scalpels and needles used to deliver them.

( D ) Example: To harvest the horseshoe crabs’ blood, the animals are caught and sent to one of a small number of laboratories.

There they are cleaned, restrained, and drained of one third of their blood with a needle. 23

That is the good news. The bad news is that horseshoe crab populations overall aren’t doing very well. 24 Although a synthetic substitute for horseshoe crab blood has been available for medical use since 2003, not all countries have chosen to use it. It is also considered by some to be slightly less accurate than the crabs’ blood in detecting toxins.

All of this means that conservationists and the medical establishment, at least in some countries, are battling over the use of horseshoe crabs as test kits. 25 What happens when the last of them has been so used remains an open question.
